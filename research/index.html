Research

My research is mainly focused on the econometrics of social networks and the financial econometrics. In particular, I am interested in methods for networks. In my Job market paper, I examine a social interactions model for count data. This model generalizes the rational expectation model of Lee et al. (2014) for binary outcome, and allows the dependent variable to take an unlimited number of possibilities. The model can be used to analyze peer effect on the number of occurrences of an event in a constant period (e.g., the number of cigarettes smoked, the number of times people eat fast food, go to the gym, etc.). In another paper, a joint work with Vincent Boucher, we propose a new method for estimating peer effect by releasing the common assumption that the network is entirely observed or measured without errors. This is better suited to networks constructed from survey data. Lastly, one of my papers (Accepted in Journal of Financial Econometrics) with Arnaud Dufays and Alain Coen, uses a variables selection method to detect relevant changes in the parameters of linear-in-mean autoregressive (AR and ARX) models for time series subject to multiple structural breaks.

Working Papers

  • Count Data Model with Social interactions, 2020 (Job Market Paper) [R Package]

    I use an incomplete information network game to examine a peer effect model in which, outcome takes integer values. Players simultaneously choose their strategy and get payoff by forming rational belief about the distribution of their peers' strategy. I find sufficient conditions under which the game equilibrium is unique. I also prove that the model generalizes Lee et al. (2014). The model parameters can be estimated using the Nested Partial Likelihood method. The limit distribution of the estimator is studied. In terms of dispersion fitting, the model is more flexible than most standard count data models. As empirical application, I study peer effect on the number of extracurricular activities in which students are enrolled. In this empirical exercise, I control for the network endogeneity.



  • Estimating Peer Effects using Partial Network Data (with Vincent Boucher), 2020, (Submitted in Review of Economics Studies) [Paper] [R Package]

    We study the estimation of peer effects through social networks when researchers do not observe the network structure. Instead, we assume that researchers know (have a consistent estimate of) the distribution of the network. We show that this assumption is sufficient for the estimation of peer effects using a linear-in-means model. We present and discuss important examples where our methodology can be applied. In particular, we provide an empirical application to the study of peer effects on students’ academic achievement.
